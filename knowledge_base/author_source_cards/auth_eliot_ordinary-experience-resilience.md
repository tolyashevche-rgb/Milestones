## Claim (paraphrased principle)

The useful low-pressure idea is that early development reflects both maturation and experience,
while healthy development can unfold across a wide range of ordinary family environments. The
book can help explain why everyday sensory and relational moments matter, but it is not a current
clinical source for precise brain timelines or predictions.

## Practical implication

Offer ordinary, safe variety during care and play: a familiar voice, face-to-face contact, gentle
movement, touch the child welcomes, and one object to look at or explore. Follow the child’s cues
and pause. No special “brain enrichment” programme, constant stimulation, or early-academic task
is required.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

WHO nurturing care provides the current evidence boundary: responsive caregiving and
opportunities for early learning happen through enjoyable, age-appropriate interaction. Eliot’s
usable contribution is a secondary explanatory bridge between maturation and ordinary
experience. It does not establish the effectiveness, dosage, or developmental outcome of any
specific Milestones activity.

## Allowed wording

- “Inspired by Lise Eliot’s description of development as an interaction between maturation and experience.”
- “Everyday voices, faces, movement, and safe exploration provide natural learning opportunities.”
- “Ordinary responsive moments matter; there is no need to maximise stimulation.”

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not reuse precise synapse counts, pruning rates, brain-development timetables, or “wiring”
  claims from this 2000 edition without a current primary source and expert review.
- Do not transfer enriched-versus-deprived animal findings directly to a child, or say stimulation
  makes a larger, smarter, or permanently better brain.
- Do not claim that a missed exposure, language, music activity, “critical window”, or sensory
  exercise permanently removes an ability or opportunity.
- Do not use sex-difference claims, IQ framing, early-reading pressure, or parent-guilt language.
- Claim limit: supports a calm rationale for ordinary interaction; it does not prescribe
  stimulation, predict outcomes, diagnose, prevent, or treat developmental concerns.

## Safety note

More input is not always better. Keep play brief, avoid sensory overload, and stop if the child
turns away, cries, stiffens, coughs, or appears tired. Use developmentally safe positions and
objects with awake adult supervision. Concerns about response to sound, vision, movement, or
social interaction should be discussed with a healthcare professional rather than answered with
more intensive stimulation.
