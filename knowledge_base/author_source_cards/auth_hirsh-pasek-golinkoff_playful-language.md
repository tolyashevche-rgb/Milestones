## Claim (paraphrased principle)

From the earliest months, babies participate in communication through gaze, facial expression,
movement, coos, babble, pauses, and later gestures. Warm back-and-forth interaction gives the
caregiver an everyday way to notice and respond to those signals. The useful principle is a
conversational duet, not a lesson, drill, performance, or word-count target.

## Practical implication

During an ordinary routine, notice one sound, look, movement, or gesture; respond warmly; then
pause. Around the middle of the first year, follow the baby’s vocal turn or gaze rather than
filling every silence. Toward 9–12 months, put a simple word to what the baby is already looking
at or communicating. A missed turn is ordinary, and the baby never has to repeat or perform.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

The book’s infant examples align with current Harvard serve-and-return and CDC communication
milestones: sounds back at 4 months, sound-taking turns at 6 months, varied babble at 9 months,
and meaningful gestures around 12 months. Those current sources set the evidence boundary;
*How Babies Talk* contributes detailed observation and parent-friendly framing.

## Allowed wording

- “Treat a sound, look, movement, or gesture as the baby’s turn.”
- “Respond warmly, then leave a little room for the baby’s next signal.”
- “Name what already has the baby’s attention.”
- “Inspired by Hirsh-Pasek and Golinkoff’s conversational-duet framing.”

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not say an activity makes a baby talk earlier, increases vocabulary, raises intelligence,
  builds the brain, or prevents a language delay.
- Do not turn the book’s “Try This” observations into tests, repeat them to elicit a response, or
  infer ability from whether the baby looks, imitates, vocalizes, points, or responds on cue.
- Do not deliberately ignore, interrupt, misunderstand, frustrate, or withhold a wanted object in
  order to “test” communication.
- Do not demand eye contact, imitation, a word, a gesture, or a correct response; following the
  baby’s attention must not become directing it.
- Do not prescribe a special voice, constant narration, a minimum number of words, or continuous
  interaction. Quiet, multilingual families and caregivers with limited energy are not deficient.
- Do not present dated age descriptions in the book as individual deadlines or screening cutoffs.
- Claim limit: supports low-pressure communication opportunities; it does not test hearing or
  language, treat delay, predict vocabulary, or guarantee an outcome.

## Safety note

Use only when the baby is awake and receptive; pause when they turn away, become distressed, or
need care. Do not make loud sounds near the baby or use response to a home sound as a hearing
test. A concern about hearing, communication, or loss of a skill belongs in the existing “Ask”
path and with a qualified professional.
