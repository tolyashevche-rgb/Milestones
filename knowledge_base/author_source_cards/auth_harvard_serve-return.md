## Claim (paraphrased principle)

Young children learn through repeated back-and-forth exchanges with responsive adults.
When a baby looks, moves, vocalizes, reaches, smiles, fusses, or points, the adult can
notice the signal, respond warmly, name what is happening, wait for another turn, and
keep the interaction brief enough to stay comfortable.

## Practical implication

Parent activities should include a pause. The adult does not need a special toy or a
lesson plan: they can copy a sound, return a smile, name an object, wait, and respond
again during feeding, diapering, dressing, floor play, or cuddling.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

This expresses the serve-and-return mechanism: developmentally useful interaction is
responsive, contingent, and reciprocal. It aligns with WHO nurturing care because it
supports responsive caregiving and early learning, and it aligns with CDC social and
language milestones because many early milestones are observable during everyday
turn-taking routines.

## Allowed wording

- "Back-and-forth interaction supports early communication and social attention."
- "Try one warm response, then pause and wait for your baby's next signal."
- "This is aligned with Harvard's serve-and-return mechanism."

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not say this guarantees language, intelligence, attachment security, or faster milestones.
- Do not imply that parents must respond perfectly every time.
- Claim limit: supports developmental conditions and practice opportunities; does not diagnose, treat, or replace professional guidance.

## Safety note

Stop if the child turns away, cries, stiffens, coughs, seems tired, or becomes
overstimulated. If a parent is worried that a child rarely responds to sound, voice,
face, name, or social play, suggest discussing the concern with a healthcare
professional.
