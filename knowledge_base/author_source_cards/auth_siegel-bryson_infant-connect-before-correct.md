## Claim (paraphrased principle)

The infant-safe part of the authors’ relationship-first framing is for the adult to slow their
own reaction, notice the baby’s signal, and make calm contact before deciding what the baby may
need. For a baby aged 0–12 months, crying or fussing is communication: there is nothing to
discipline or redirect.

## Practical implication

The caregiver can take one breath, check immediate safety and basic needs, soften their voice,
come close without overwhelming the baby, and respond to what they observe. A brief pause is for
reducing the adult’s reactivity, not for delaying feeding, comfort, medical attention, or another
needed response. If the caregiver is overwhelmed, they can place the baby in a safe place and ask
another trusted adult or a qualified professional for support.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

WHO responsive caregiving and Harvard serve-and-return provide the current evidence boundary:
adults notice and respond to a young child’s signals through warm, contingent interaction.
Siegel and Bryson contribute an accessible relationship-first reminder, not the scientific
mechanism. The book’s discipline model and simplified brain metaphors are not transferred into
the Milestones infant experience.

## Allowed wording

- “First connect with the baby’s signal; there is nothing to correct.”
- “A calm voice and a responsive pause can support co-regulation.”
- “Crying and fussing are signals, not misbehavior.”
- “Inspired by Siegel and Bryson’s relationship-first framing.”

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not describe infant crying, fussing, waking, feeding needs, or reaching for a caregiver as
  manipulation, misbehavior, a tantrum, or a discipline problem.
- Do not prescribe correction, consequences, lessons, ignoring, or “connect and redirect” as a
  behavior-management sequence for a child aged 0–12 months.
- Do not present left/right brain, upstairs/downstairs brain, integration, rewiring, or mirror
  neurons as literal explanations for an infant activity or caregiver response.
- Do not claim that one response will calm the baby, create secure attachment, change the brain,
  prevent future behavior, or produce a developmental outcome.
- Do not tell caregivers they must always be calm or blame them for becoming reactive, needing a
  pause, or asking for help.
- Claim limit: supports a gentle response to an infant signal; it is not a discipline method,
  neuroscience lesson, attachment assessment, or treatment for caregiver or infant distress.

## Safety note

Connection-first wording must never delay feeding, a pain or illness check, safe-sleep action,
supervision, or professional care. Never shake or restrain a baby. If the adult feels unable to
stay in control, place the baby safely and seek immediate help from a trusted adult or qualified
local service. Milestones must not act as crisis care.
