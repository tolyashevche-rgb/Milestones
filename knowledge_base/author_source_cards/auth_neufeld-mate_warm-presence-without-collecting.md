## Claim (paraphrased principle)

The only infant-safe reusable form is to offer warm presence without making the baby perform for
connection. A caregiver may greet the baby, come close, use a gentle voice or offer touch, while
noticing and respecting whether the baby engages, pauses or turns away. The adult responds to the
baby’s needs; they do not “collect”, test, secure or diagnose attachment.

## Practical implication

After an ordinary separation or before a routine, the caregiver can slow down, make themselves
available and notice the baby’s next signal. Eye contact, a smile, raised arms, a grasp or physical
affection are never required. If the baby does not reciprocate, the moment is still complete; the
caregiver may give space and attend to feeding, sleep, comfort, safety or another need.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

WHO responsive caregiving and Harvard serve-and-return provide the current boundary: notice,
understand and respond appropriately to the child’s signal. *Hold On to Your Kids* contributes
only a relationship-before-behavior tone. Its attachment hierarchy, “collecting”, dependence and
peer-orientation model is not treated as current infant evidence and is not transferred into
Milestones.

## Allowed wording

- “Offer a warm hello and leave room for the baby’s response—or no response.”
- “Connection is an invitation, not something the baby must demonstrate.”
- “A turned-away gaze can be a cue for a pause, not a relationship verdict.”
- “Inspired only by Neufeld and Maté’s relationship-first tone.”

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not use “collect the child”, “woo”, “get in the child’s face”, “invite dependence”,
  “attachment dance”, “compass point”, or other strategic-control language with caregivers.
- Do not try to elicit or require eye contact, a smile, nod, grasp, raised arms, cuddle or physical
  proximity before providing care, comfort, feeding, safety or an activity.
- Do not interpret gaze, smiling, reaching, grasping, crying, separation response, touch preference
  or lack of response as secure/insecure attachment, readiness to depend, rejection, manipulation
  or a relationship problem.
- Do not claim that parental contact prevents later demanding behavior, creates independence,
  protects maturity, or determines peer relationships, learning, aggression or mental health.
- Do not apply peer-orientation, competing-attachment or parent-replacement claims to babies,
  siblings, childcare, other trusted caregivers, returning to work or ordinary separations.
- Do not prescribe hierarchy, authority, command, obedience, deference or dependence as an infant
  developmental mechanism.
- Do not blame caregivers for needing childcare, work, rest, boundaries or support, or for stress,
  imperfect warmth, missed greetings, separation, relationship difficulty or limited capacity.
- Claim limit: optional warm-presence form only; no attachment assessment, parenting method,
  behavioral control, separation prescription, childcare judgment, diagnosis or outcome claim.

## Safety note

Physical closeness must be safe and welcomed; never hold, restrain, crowd or pursue a baby to get
a response. Connection language must never delay feeding, sleep, medical attention or protection.
Babies can form relationships with multiple safe caregivers. If the adult is overwhelmed, it is
appropriate to place the baby safely and seek support. Milestones does not assess attachment or
provide relationship treatment.
