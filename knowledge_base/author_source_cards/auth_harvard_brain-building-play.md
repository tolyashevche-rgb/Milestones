## Claim (paraphrased principle)

Play can be used as a practical developmental context: children practice attention,
memory, turn-taking, flexible thinking, self-control, communication, and relationship
skills through simple games and playful routines that match their age and state.

## Practical implication

Activities should be short, ordinary, and age-matched. For infants, the adult stays
actively involved: face games, sound copying, peekaboo, naming, hiding-and-finding,
and simple turn-taking routines. For older children, the adult can gradually create
space for more independent play, choice, waiting, remembering rules, and flexible
problem-solving.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

The card maps play to two mechanisms: responsive caregiving and executive-function
practice. Play is not used as proof that a milestone will appear; it is used as a
low-pressure way to create repeated opportunities for attention, social exchange,
movement, communication, and regulation.

## Allowed wording

- "This play gives your child a chance to practice attention and turn-taking."
- "Keep it short and follow your child's interest."
- "Play supports development when it is responsive, safe, and matched to the child's age."

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not promise that a game will accelerate development or produce a specific outcome.
- Do not turn play into testing, drilling, scoring, or performance pressure.
- Claim limit: supports practice opportunities and relationship quality; does not measure development or replace clinical advice.

## Safety note

Choose safe materials, supervise closely, and stop if the child is tired,
overstimulated, frustrated, hungry, or uncomfortable. For infant activities, avoid
small objects, unsafe sleep positions, forced positions, and any activity that creates
stress for the child or caregiver.
