## Claim (paraphrased principle)

The safe, useful idea is that parenting evidence rarely supplies one perfect response for every
child and family. A caregiver can observe the child in front of them, use broad safety and
responsive-care principles, and aim for a workable “good enough” response rather than constant
optimization. Caregivers also need practical and relational support.

## Practical implication

Reduce the next step to one manageable action: notice one signal, respond warmly, and pause. If
the caregiver is depleted, another trusted adult can help with a routine, a meal, rest, or simply
staying nearby. Milestones should make pausing and returning normal and must not frame app
completion as proof of good parenting.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

WHO nurturing care treats caregiver capacity and responsive caregiving as connected and states
that parents need support. Harvard likewise links caregiver well-being and supportive conditions
with the capacity for responsive relationships. Medina contributes a parent-friendly reminder
about variation and the limits of one-size-fits-all advice; the primary frameworks provide the
current evidence boundary.

## Allowed wording

- “There is no single perfect response for every family.”
- “One warm, manageable response is enough for this moment.”
- “Caregivers need support too; asking for help is compatible with responsive care.”
- “Inspired by John Medina’s caution that parenting research rarely settles every specific choice.”

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not claim there is one parenting style proven to produce smart, happy, moral, or successful
  children.
- Do not reuse IQ gains, genetic percentages, academic predictors, exact brain effects, or
  “optimize/build a better brain” language from the book without current primary evidence and
  expert review.
- Do not say caregiver calmness, relationship structure, household chores, or social support
  directly determines a child’s brain or future.
- Do not blame a caregiver for stress, depression, isolation, missed routines, imperfect
  responsiveness, or needing to pause.
- Claim limit: supports a low-pressure product tone and help-seeking; it does not assess parenting,
  prescribe family structure, diagnose caregiver distress, or predict child outcomes.

## Safety note

“Good enough” does not relax physical safety, supervision, feeding, sleep, or professional-care
guidance. If distress feels unmanageable or anyone’s immediate safety may be at risk, contact a
qualified health professional or local urgent support rather than relying on the app. Milestones
must not act as crisis care.
