## Claim (paraphrased principle)

The only infant-safe reusable form is an optional shared look at one ordinary picture, book page,
or safe object at the baby's pace. A caregiver may name what the baby notices, pause, and follow
the baby's response or stop cue. Current responsive-interaction guidance supports that form;
Shichida materials do not establish that rapid or massive input activates a hemisphere, unlocks
hidden ability, creates memory, or accelerates learning.

## Practical implication

If the baby is awake and interested, the caregiver may hold one picture or object comfortably in
view, name it once in ordinary speech, wait, and respond to any look, sound, reach, turn-away, or
need for care. There is no card deck, speed, quota, repetition schedule, test, correct answer, or
expected result. Looking away or stopping is a successful cue, not a missed learning opportunity.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

WHO responsive caregiving and Harvard serve-and-return support noticing and answering a child's
signals during ordinary play and communication. Those sources, not the Shichida method, provide
the mechanism and boundary. Neuroimaging research recognizes local functional lateralization but
does not support a global left-brained/right-brained person. That study is not infant efficacy
evidence; the reviewed materials themselves provide no credible infant evidence for hemisphere
activation, alpha-state learning, photographic memory, extraordinary calculation, or extrasensory
perception.

## Allowed wording

- "If the baby is interested, share one picture and name what they notice."
- "Pause and follow the baby's pace; looking away means it is time to stop or change."
- "This is a shared conversation, not memory or brain training."
- "The simple picture-sharing form is retained; Shichida's right-brain claims are not."

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not say the right brain dominates from birth to age three, that the left brain later replaces
  it, or that a caregiver can activate, awaken, synchronize, balance, or train either hemisphere.
- Do not use alpha/beta waves, subconscious input, deep breathing, meditation, "alpha music",
  energy balls, relaxation, speed, rhythm, or high-volume input as a learning mechanism.
- Do not prescribe rapid flash cards, dots, numerals, equations, words, flags, encyclopedic facts,
  image sequences, tapes, videos, mandalas, eye exercises, forced visual tracking, or card speeds,
  counts, sessions, repetitions, curricula, or commercial materials.
- Do not test recognition, guessing, memory, card order, colours, shapes, dots, arithmetic, mental
  images, prenatal memories, intuition, or a caregiver's imagined "sent" picture.
- Do not claim photographic memory, instant comprehension, speed reading, perfect pitch, foreign
  language, vocabulary, calculation, creativity, intelligence, IQ, genius, concentration, school
  success, unlimited potential, or easier future learning.
- Do not mention resonance, quantum/subatomic vibration, skin receptors, clairvoyance, telepathy,
  telekinesis, precognition, ESP, womb communication, or prenatal learning as established fact.
- Do not claim the method changes facial muscles, brain pathways, disability, Down syndrome,
  developmental delay, vision, neurological function, behaviour, health, or any diagnosis.
- Do not say zero to three is a golden, critical, easiest, or last-chance period; do not imply that
  slow input is inferior or that a caregiver must maximize knowledge before an ability disappears.
- Do not blame a caregiver's pace, consistency, emotional state, eye contact, love, materials,
  missed sessions, limited time, or decision not to train for a child's ability or future.
- Claim limit: optional shared-picture form only; no Shichida method, flash cards, dose, brain
  mechanism, assessment, treatment, prediction, extraordinary ability, or outcome claim.

## Safety note

Use only a stable, age-safe picture, book, or object while the baby is awake and comfortably
supported. Do not flash bright lights or rapidly changing screens, force gaze or head movement,
hold material close to the eyes, or continue through turning away, blinking, distress, fatigue, or
overstimulation. Picture materials must not replace free movement, sleep, feeding, responsive
conversation, or ordinary play. Concerns about vision, hearing, development, seizures, or unusual
responses belong with a qualified health professional, not a home test or training program.
