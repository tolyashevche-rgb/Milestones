## Claim (paraphrased principle)

The only reusable form is flexible, nonjudgmental responsiveness: notice a baby's signal, offer
appropriate care when possible, adjust when the first response does not help, and protect the
caregiver's capacity. This form is supported by current responsive-care guidance. Sears's
attachment-parenting system is not evidence that a checklist, feeding method, carrier, sleep
arrangement, or response speed creates attachment, intelligence, independence, health, or a
better future.

## Practical implication

Milestones may invite a caregiver to pause, notice a sound, look, movement, hunger cue, discomfort,
or need for rest, and offer one calm response. The baby does not have to calm, smile, look back, or
accept contact. Another safe caregiver may respond, and the adult may place the baby in a safe
sleep space and seek support when exhausted or overwhelmed. No "Baby B" is a requirement or a
measure of love, attachment, or parenting quality.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

WHO responsive caregiving and Harvard serve-and-return provide the current mechanism: notice and
respond to a child's signals in ordinary interaction without demanding a particular result. AAP
and CPSC guidance override the book wherever sleep surfaces, carriers, or other safety issues are
involved. Sears contributes only a warm cue-responsive tone; his method, medical advice, causal
claims, and outcome promises are not transferred into Milestones.

## Allowed wording

- "Notice the baby's cue and try one calm response; it is okay to adjust or ask for help."
- "Responsiveness can come from any safe, familiar caregiver."
- "You do not need to follow a parenting checklist to care warmly for your baby."
- "Inspired only by Sears's warm responsive-care tone, not by attachment-parenting rules."

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not present birth bonding, immediate response to every cry, breastfeeding, babywearing,
  bedding close, balance/boundaries, or avoiding "baby trainers" as a seven-part checklist,
  attachment test, moral duty, or superior parenting method.
- Do not recommend bed sharing or call it safe. Do not reproduce the book's adult-bed guardrail,
  wall, rolled-blanket, mattress-on-floor, waterbed, large-bed, or parent-awareness precautions.
  Room sharing means a separate, firm, flat infant sleep surface with only a fitted sheet.
- Do not claim attachment parenting, bed sharing, breastfeeding, carrying, prompt soothing, or
  any Milestones activity prevents or lowers SIDS risk.
- Do not claim carrying makes babies cry less, redirects energy into growth, improves motor or
  brain development, prevents caregiver depression, or creates attachment. Do not prescribe hours
  in a carrier or treat a sling as a sleep surface.
- Do not imply breastfeeding creates better bonding, mothering hormones, intelligence, health,
  sleep, or parenting success. Never frame formula, combination feeding, pumping, weaning,
  adoption, surrogacy, or inability or choice not to breastfeed as weaker care or attachment.
- Do not label another sleep approach as detached, insensitive, inhumane, trust-breaking, or
  harmful to attachment. Do not prescribe sleep training or infer relationship quality from sleep,
  crying, settling, contact, feeding, or separation.
- Do not claim the method improves behavior, development, intelligence, language, independence,
  discipline, intimacy, trust, competence, brain development, growth, or future relationships.
- Do not privilege maternal intuition or a birth mother's response over another caregiver, assign
  care roles by gender, or blame a caregiver's fatigue, work, boundaries, feeding, sleep choice,
  delayed response, mood, or need for space.
- Do not reuse vaccination schedules, medication doses, diagnoses, treatments, product advice, or
  other medical guidance from the 2013 book. Current qualified clinical guidance always governs.
- Claim limit: optional responsive-care tone only; no method, checklist, sleep or feeding advice,
  attachment assessment, medical claim, dose, diagnosis, treatment, prediction, or outcome claim.

## Safety note

For every sleep, place the baby on their back on a firm, flat, separate infant sleep surface with
only a fitted sheet. If a caregiver might fall asleep while feeding or comforting, follow current
AAP harm-reduction guidance and return the baby to their own sleep space as soon as the caregiver
wakes. Never sleep with a baby on a sofa or armchair. In a carrier, keep the baby's face visible
and unobstructed, keep the chin off the chest, check breathing often, and move a sleeping baby to a
safe sleep surface. Feeding, sleep, pain, illness, medication, vaccination, or growth concerns
belong with a qualified health professional. Caregiver exhaustion deserves practical support, not
guilt or a stricter method.
