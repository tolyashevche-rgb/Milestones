## Claim (paraphrased principle)

Stamm organizes early everyday care around three broad ideas: giving the child calm attention,
responding warmly and predictably, and communicating during ordinary routines. Milestones may
borrow this low-pressure form, but the evidence boundary comes from responsive-caregiving and
serve-and-return sources rather than from the author framework itself.

## Practical implication

Use one brief everyday exchange: notice a look, sound, movement, or pause; respond with your face,
voice, touch, or a few words; then wait for the child’s next signal. Feeding, dressing, cuddling,
and floor play are enough. The parent does not need a lesson plan or perfect consistency.

## Why it maps to the mechanism

The usable part is a practical wrapper for responsive caregiving: an adult notices the child’s
signal, answers contingently, and leaves room for another turn. This aligns with Harvard’s
serve-and-return mechanism and WHO nurturing care components of responsive caregiving and early
learning. Those primary frameworks support the mechanism; Stamm contributes only a memorable
way to organize ordinary interactions.

## Allowed wording

- “Inspired by Jill Stamm’s focus on simple everyday attention and communication.”
- “A short back-and-forth moment supports early communication and social attention.”
- “Notice one signal, respond warmly, and pause for the next turn.”

## Not allowed wording / claim limit

- Do not claim that this “wires the brain correctly”, increases IQ, lengthens attention span,
  secures attachment, accelerates milestones, or predicts later school success.
- Do not present “Attention, Bonding, and Communication” as a clinically validated programme or
  developmental prescription.
- Do not imply that occasional missed signals, low energy, work, stress, or imperfect routines
  harm the child.
- Claim limit: offers responsive interaction practice; it does not diagnose, prevent, or treat a
  developmental concern.

## Safety note

Keep the exchange brief and follow the child’s state. Pause or stop if the child turns away,
cries, stiffens, coughs, or seems tired or overstimulated. Concerns about limited response to
sound, voice, faces, or social interaction belong in a conversation with a healthcare
professional, not in more intensive practice.
