## Parent Promise

At 6 months, your baby may be getting more social, more curious and more active with their body. This page helps you notice what is emerging, choose simple play for the week, and know when to ask a professional.

This is not a test. It is a calm observation and play guide.

## Quick Disclaimer

This page supports developmental monitoring and playful learning. It is not a diagnostic or screening tool and does not replace advice from your child's healthcare provider.

## Play: Review-Ready Activity Cards

### 1. Sound Turn-Taking

Metadata:

- Activity ID: `act_006_language_001`
- Domain: language/communication
- Time: 2-5 minutes
- Materials: none
- Supports: `ms_006_language_001`, `ms_006_language_002`
- Evidence label: Mechanism-based
- Source IDs: `harvard_serve_return_guide`, `who_nurturing_care`
- Claim limit: Supports communication prerequisites. Does not treat or diagnose language delay.

Steps:

1. Hold baby where they can see your face.
2. Wait for a sound, smile, kick, or expression.
3. Copy it warmly.
4. Pause and give baby time to respond.

What to watch:

- baby vocalizes;
- baby smiles or kicks;
- baby looks, pauses, or changes expression.

Why it works:

Back-and-forth interaction supports attention, early communication and social learning. This aligns with the serve-and-return mechanism described by Harvard Center on the Developing Child.

Stop rules:

Stop if baby turns away, cries, stiffens, coughs, or seems tired.

Low-energy option:

Do one 30-second sound exchange during diapering.

Expert review notes:

- Confirm that wording is appropriate for parent-facing language development support.
- Confirm whether hearing-related discuss prompt should be stronger if baby makes very few sounds.

### 2. Mirror Hello

Metadata:

- Activity ID: `act_006_social_002`
- Domain: social/emotional
- Time: 2-4 minutes
- Materials: baby-safe mirror
- Supports: `ms_006_social_002`, `ms_006_social_003`
- Evidence label: Mechanism-based
- Source IDs: `cdc_ltsae_6_months`, `harvard_serve_return_guide`
- Claim limit: Supports social attention and interaction. Does not measure self-recognition or attachment quality.

Steps:

1. Sit with baby supported and facing a baby-safe mirror.
2. Say hello to baby's reflection.
3. Smile, pause and notice what baby does.
4. Name what you see: "You are looking. You smiled."

What to watch:

- baby looks at the reflection;
- baby smiles, touches, vocalizes, or turns away.

Why it works:

Face-focused routines support social attention and responsive caregiving. The goal is interaction, not performance.

Stop rules:

Use a baby-safe mirror. Stop if baby becomes overstimulated or uncomfortable.

Low-energy option:

One short "hello" to the mirror is enough.

Expert review notes:

- Confirm mirror safety wording.
- Confirm parent tone does not imply self-recognition is expected.

### 3. Side Reach Toy

Metadata:

- Activity ID: `act_006_cognitive_001`
- Domain: cognitive + movement/physical
- Time: 3-5 minutes
- Materials: one safe toy
- Supports: `ms_006_cognitive_002`, `ms_006_movement_003`
- Evidence label: Mechanism-based
- Source IDs: `cdc_ltsae_6_months`, `who_motor_milestones`
- Claim limit: Supports reaching practice and exploration. Does not require grasping every time.

Steps:

1. Place baby on back, side-lying, tummy, or supported sitting.
2. Hold a toy slightly to one side.
3. Wait for looking, reaching, or body shift.
4. Celebrate the attempt, even if baby does not grab it.

What to watch:

- baby looks toward the toy;
- baby reaches or shifts body;
- baby uses one or both hands.

Why it works:

Reaching practice supports visual attention, arm control, body awareness and early problem-solving.

Stop rules:

Avoid small or choking-risk items. Stop if baby is tired or frustrated.

Low-energy option:

Offer one side reach once during floor time.

Expert review notes:

- Confirm safe positions for 5-7 months.
- Confirm asymmetry referral wording.

### 4. Straight-Arm Tummy Reach

Metadata:

- Activity ID: `act_006_movement_001`
- Domain: movement/physical
- Time: 2-5 minutes
- Materials: blanket and safe toy
- Supports: `ms_006_movement_002`, `ms_006_cognitive_002`
- Evidence label: Mechanism-based
- Source IDs: `cdc_ltsae_6_months`, `who_motor_milestones`
- Claim limit: Supports motor practice. Does not force milestone achievement.

Steps:

1. Place baby awake and supervised on tummy.
2. Put a toy in front and slightly to the side.
3. Encourage looking, reaching, or pushing up.
4. Keep it brief and repeat later if baby is comfortable.

What to watch:

- baby pushes up;
- baby shifts weight;
- baby reaches, watches, or turns head.

Why it works:

Brief supervised prone play supports head, shoulder and trunk control, which are part of early motor development.

Stop rules:

Only during awake supervised play. Stop if baby is distressed, tired, hungry, or uncomfortable. Never use tummy play during sleep.

Low-energy option:

One short tummy reach moment is enough.

Expert review notes:

- Movement specialist should confirm positioning language.
- Confirm whether "straight-arm" should be softened for parent understanding.

### 5. Responsive Feeding Pause

Metadata:

- Activity ID: `act_006_cognitive_002`
- Domain: cognitive + social/emotional
- Time: during feeding
- Materials: normal feeding setup
- Supports: `ms_006_cognitive_003`
- Evidence label: Nurturing-care aligned
- Source IDs: `cdc_ltsae_6_months`, `who_nurturing_care`
- Claim limit: Supports cue reading and responsive caregiving. Does not solve feeding problems.

Steps:

1. Offer food or milk as usual.
2. Pause when baby slows down, closes mouth, or turns away.
3. Name the cue calmly: "You are telling me all done."
4. Continue only if baby re-engages.

What to watch:

- baby opens mouth, closes mouth, turns away, reaches, relaxes, or becomes tense.

Why it works:

Responsive caregiving helps baby's cues become meaningful in interaction. This supports trust, regulation and early communication.

Stop rules:

If feeding is stressful, painful, unsafe, or growth is a concern, speak with a healthcare professional.

Low-energy option:

Pause once and name the cue.

Expert review notes:

- Feeding safety review required.
- Confirm whether this belongs in milestone product or should be optional.

## Monitor / Discuss With Professional

Consider discussing with a healthcare provider if:

- your baby has lost a skill they previously had;
- you have concerns about hearing, vision, feeding, movement, or interaction;
- one side of the body seems much weaker or less active;
- baby is not making sounds, not responding to familiar people, or not reaching for objects and you are concerned;
- tummy play, rolling, or supported sitting feels unusually difficult.

Parent-friendly wording:

If you do not see a milestone, that does not automatically mean something is wrong. It means: observe, support through play, and discuss concerns early rather than waiting in worry.
