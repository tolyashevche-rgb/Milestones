# 6 Months Sample Page: CDC x Europe Milestones

Status: Stage 2 sample
Age window: 5-7 months
Primary sources: CDC 6 months, AAP milestone methodology, WHO motor milestones, WHO nurturing care, Harvard serve-and-return.

## Parent Promise

At 6 months, your baby may be getting more social, more curious and more active with their body. This page helps you notice what is emerging, choose simple play for the week, and know when to ask a professional.

This is not a test. It is a calm observation and play guide.

## Quick Disclaimer

This page supports developmental monitoring and playful learning. It is not a diagnostic or screening tool and does not replace advice from your child's healthcare provider.

## Watch: Milestones To Observe

### Social & Emotional

| Milestone | What It May Look Like | Try This |
|---|---|---|
| Recognizes familiar people | Baby responds differently to people they know well. | Familiar face greeting |
| Enjoys mirror interaction | Baby looks at or reacts to their reflection. | Mirror hello |
| Laughs during play | Baby laughs or shows clear joy in interaction. | Laugh-and-pause play |

### Language & Communication

| Milestone | What It May Look Like | Try This |
|---|---|---|
| Takes turns with sounds | Baby makes a sound, waits, or responds after you copy it. | Sound turn-taking |
| Explores mouth sounds | Baby makes playful sounds such as bubbles, squeals, or raspberries. | Silly sound copy |

### Learning & Thinking

| Milestone | What It May Look Like | Try This |
|---|---|---|
| Explores objects with mouth | Baby safely mouths toys to learn about shape, texture and sensation. | Safe texture explore |
| Reaches for wanted toy | Baby reaches toward a toy or object they want. | Side reach toy |
| Shows stopping cue during feeding | Baby closes mouth, turns away, or pauses when they do not want more. | Responsive feeding pause |

### Movement & Physical

| Milestone | What It May Look Like | Try This |
|---|---|---|
| Rolls from tummy toward back | Baby rolls or starts rolling from tummy to back. | Rolling invitation |
| Pushes up during tummy play | Baby pushes chest up with straighter arms while awake on tummy. | Straight-arm tummy reach |
| Leans on hands while sitting | Baby uses hands to support the body in sitting. | Tripod sit practice |

## Play: Activity Cards

### 1. Sound Turn-Taking

- Domain: language/communication
- Time: 2-5 minutes
- Materials: none
- Supports: taking turns with sounds
- Evidence label: mechanism-based

Steps:

1. Hold baby where they can see your face.
2. Wait for a sound, smile, kick, or expression.
3. Copy it warmly.
4. Pause and give baby time to respond.

Why it works:

Back-and-forth interaction supports attention, early communication and social learning. This aligns with the serve-and-return mechanism described by Harvard Center on the Developing Child.

Stop rules:

Stop if baby turns away, cries, stiffens, coughs, or seems tired.

Low-energy option:

Do one 30-second sound exchange during diapering.

### 2. Mirror Hello

- Domain: social/emotional
- Time: 2-4 minutes
- Materials: baby-safe mirror
- Supports: recognizing faces and enjoying social interaction
- Evidence label: mechanism-based

Steps:

1. Sit with baby supported and facing a baby-safe mirror.
2. Say hello to baby's reflection.
3. Smile, pause and notice what baby does.
4. Name what you see: "You are looking. You smiled."

Why it works:

Face-focused routines support social attention and responsive caregiving. The goal is interaction, not performance.

Stop rules:

Stop if baby becomes overstimulated or uncomfortable.

### 3. Side Reach Toy

- Domain: cognitive + movement/physical
- Time: 3-5 minutes
- Materials: one safe toy
- Supports: reaching for wanted toy and shifting attention/body
- Evidence label: mechanism-based

Steps:

1. Place baby on back, side-lying, or supported sitting.
2. Hold a toy slightly to one side.
3. Wait for looking, reaching, or body shift.
4. Celebrate the attempt, even if baby does not grab it.

Why it works:

Reaching practice supports visual attention, arm control, body awareness and early problem-solving.

Stop rules:

Avoid small/choking items. Stop if baby is tired or frustrated.

### 4. Straight-Arm Tummy Reach

- Domain: movement/physical
- Time: 2-5 minutes
- Materials: blanket, safe toy
- Supports: pushing up during tummy play
- Evidence label: guideline-aligned + mechanism-based

Steps:

1. Place baby awake and supervised on tummy.
2. Put a toy in front and slightly to the side.
3. Encourage looking, reaching, or pushing up.
4. Keep it brief and repeat later if baby is comfortable.

Why it works:

Brief supervised prone play supports head, shoulder and trunk control, which are part of early motor development.

Stop rules:

Only during awake supervised play. Stop if baby is distressed, tired, hungry, or uncomfortable.

### 5. Responsive Feeding Pause

- Domain: cognitive + social/emotional
- Time: during feeding
- Materials: normal feeding setup
- Supports: noticing baby's "more" and "all done" cues
- Evidence label: nurturing-care aligned

Steps:

1. Offer food or milk as usual.
2. Pause when baby slows down, closes mouth, or turns away.
3. Name the cue calmly: "You are telling me all done."
4. Continue only if baby re-engages.

Why it works:

Responsive caregiving helps baby experience communication and bodily cues as meaningful. This supports trust, regulation and early communication.

Stop rules:

If feeding is stressful, painful, unsafe, or growth is a concern, speak with a healthcare professional.

## 7-Day Plan

| Day | Focus | Activities | Low-Energy Version |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Face and sound | Sound Turn-Taking + Mirror Hello | 30 seconds of sound copying |
| 2 | Reaching | Side Reach Toy | Put one toy slightly to the side once |
| 3 | Tummy strength | Straight-Arm Tummy Reach | One short tummy moment |
| 4 | Social joy | Laugh-and-pause play + mirror | Smile, wait, respond |
| 5 | Feeding cues | Responsive Feeding Pause | Pause once and name the cue |
| 6 | Explore safely | Safe texture explore + reach | One safe object, supervised |
| 7 | Review | Repeat favorite activity + check notes | Mark one observation only |

## Monitor / Discuss With Professional

Consider discussing with a healthcare provider if:

- your baby has lost a skill they previously had;
- you have concerns about hearing, vision, feeding, movement, or interaction;
- one side of the body seems much weaker or less active;
- baby is not making sounds, not responding to familiar people, or not reaching for objects and you are concerned;
- tummy play, rolling, or supported sitting feels unusually difficult.

Parent-friendly wording:

If you do not see a milestone, that does not automatically mean something is wrong. It means: observe, support through play, and discuss concerns early rather than waiting in worry.
